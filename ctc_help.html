What is the CTC Format?

The condition-transition-consequence (CTC) format is a to refine a project risk by structuring it in a clear sentence that states all of the relevant details of the risk, namely the condition(s) and consequence. The CTC sentence format is as follows:

Given that [condition or subconditions], then there is concern that (possibly) [consequence].

The condition describes the situation or context that could lead to a risk event. Depending on how complicated the risk is, the condition could be further refined into two or more subconditions to provide more detail to what exactly is causing the risk. The transition simply directs the sentence from the condition or subcondition statements into the consequence. The consequence details the potential impact or outcome on the project if the risk event occurs.

One example of how to use the CTC format can be exemplified with the general risk of not completing a product by deployment date. This can happen for a multitude of reasons, but let's assume the concern for this project is caused by an inexperienced development team. We can use the CTC format to refine the risk as follows:

Given that the project's software development team lacks experience, then there is concern that (possibly) the product will not be delivered by the customer's deadline.

We can further refine the condition of the risk into two subconditions: one having to do with the team consisting of many new members who haven't gotten the chance to work together much, and one having to do with the team's general lack of knowledge around designing the certain type of product requested by the customers.

Given that the project's software development team has not yet found a comfortable team workflow and is unfamiliar with the type of product being designed, then there is concern that (possibly) the product will not be delivered by the customer's deadline.

Using the CTC format to refine our risk has allowed us to clearly state the details of the risk and the potential cause and effect relation between certain project conditions and an unwanted consequence. Now we know what conditions are creating this risk, and we can develop ways to prevent and deal with the risk early on in the project so that we can avoid the negative outcome and, in the worst case scenario where it happens anyway, know what to do to keep it from getting worse.

The benefit of refining your risks with the CTC format is making them easier to analyze. It encourages you to explicitly state all the project conditions which may lead to a certain consequence. Making these known means you can develop effective mitigation, monitoring, and managing strategies for all aspects of the risk.
